116 reasons to quit tech

and become a goat farmer

Notes recovered from an on‑call week—when the pager wouldn't stop and the goats started to make a lot of sense.

A classic from the server room.

Language and sarcasm ahead. This is satire—written by an exhausted techie who briefly imagined swapping 32‑bit cores for billy goats.
  • You don’t have to monitor utilization on a goat.
  • Milk a goat and it stays milked—for a while.
  • There are no 32‑bit goats.
  • No demos. If you ever do one, the goat just… works.
  • When a goat goes down, you bury it and buy another.
  • Left alone, billys and nannies do their jobs—no formatting, tracing or register inspection required.
  • Nobody cares if you’re not a Certified Goat Engineer.
  • Goat → steak is a one‑way state transition.
  • People will take a fence‑painting tip from a farmer faster than from a computer weenie.
  • No one can pad a résumé with fake goat experience.
  • Goats don’t page you.
  • When it comes to “software” (food), everything is compatible with goats.
  • No “Goat 98” upgrade to fix “Goat 95.”
  • You can tell whether a goat has been debugged by looking at it.
  • Goats don’t go obsolete. If they do—and you didn’t neuter them—they self‑upgrade.
  • No commute.
  • Goats are cute. Computers aren’t (unless they’re Macintoshes, and those are just annoying).
  • No dress code. Ever.
  • You always have the right file permissions to milk your goat.
  • Too many timeouts? Slow performance? That’s dinner.
  • No “shareware dot com” for milking tools. You either have a bucket or you don’t.
  • The bucket leaks or it doesn’t. No networks, ownership checks or checksums required.
  • No “freeing up megs” before you milk.
  • You get callouses—the way God intended.
  • No staff meetings to standardize goat‑milking procedure.
  • Nanny goats ship with zero TCP/IP and still deliver milk.
  • Most barnyard animals are fault‑tolerant (some cows excluded).
  • No weekend sign‑ins, badge swipes, or corporate coffee. If the pot’s burning, you yell at your spouse.
  • No risk you’ve been milking an improperly labeled “development goat.”
  • No preferred vendors. Context is always correct. No passwords. No intruder‑detection locks.
  • Carpal tunnel is guaranteed. Don’t worry about it.
  • Goats ship with all patches they’ll ever need. Otherwise: steak.
  • Full goats do automatic core dumps, self‑reset and return online. You just clean up. No defrag, no compression, no Goat‑32.
  • Unless the barn’s on fire, goats don’t care about power surges.
  • No nightly backups.
  • Every goat part shares the same interrupt and it still works.
  • A goat is a goat is a goat.
  • You never restart a goat. You do shut them down (first step in many recipes).
  • No one needs a goat‑milking requirements document.
  • You deliver applications to goats; not the other way round.
  • Goats will do almost anything to get comfortable. Computers will loop an error forever, no matter how hard you smack the monitor.
  • No logoff jingle while the “Exit Goat” animation plays.
  • Your phone bill won’t reveal you over‑milked your goat.
  • On a goat, SYS$ERR.LOG is always empty.
  • Operating systems come and go; goats won’t be orphaned anytime soon.
  • No workstation licensing.
  • No one minds if you milk during business hours, or reformat a goat.
  • Late shift? You’ll still see your kids—and probably recruit them to help.
  • You don’t need 32 MB of RAM to start milking.
  • Security is a stake and a rope.
  • No one ever got a General Protection Fault milking.
  • Ethernet and token‑ring goats can share a stable without a global lockup.
  • You don’t have to name goats. If you do, duplicates don’t break anything.
  • Your kids won’t meet weirdos online while playing with a goat.
  • No antitrust battle over goat‑milking buckets. No “Make this my default bucket.”
  • No desire for higher clock speed—and none available.
  • No grounding straps or anti‑static bags… unless you’re making steak.
  • There aren’t many ways to improperly shut down a goat.
  • A room of younger farmers won’t make you feel old for talking 300‑baud or 4.7 MHz goats.
  • Y2K.
  • Uninstalling a goat doesn’t require software. Maybe a gun or a knife.
  • Once the bucket is full, a goat can crash and you still won’t lose your work. Just don’t spill.
  • New goats don’t rewrite farm registry keys that upset the chickens.
  • No easter eggs in goats.
  • Your spouse will never yell at you for removing the RAM from her goat.
  • No Goat 2000, Goat Perfect 8 or Goat 123 to learn.
  • No Internal IPX Address to boot a goat.
  • No per‑bucket licensing.
  • You’ll never spend four hours upgrading a goat over the wire.
  • There is no Goat Ops.
  • Goats follow upgrade procedures.
  • Goats eat org charts.
  • If a goat gets an uncleanable virus, you shoot it.
  • If the virus is non‑terminal, the goat… handles it.
  • No pagers; no “please advise.”
  • Goats don’t care whether it’s Calcomp.
  • No one cares about remote access to the herd.
  • No MHN goat herd.
  • No one cares if the goats aren’t talking to each other.
  • Ever heard of a proprietary goat?
  • No goat analysis meetings. No goat control meetings. No meetings.
  • Goats will never need Service Pack 4.
  • No DS problems at GOATADRIVE.
  • You work the goat; the goat doesn’t work you—or your whole department.
  • A goat might bite you, but it won’t… you get the idea.
  • Forget Y2K.
  • Goats don’t ask stupid questions.
  • Goats don’t drain tech budgets that could have gone to cars.
  • If a goat dumps in the night, you clean it up when you feel like it.
  • No one fires you for connecting diskless goats to a goat server instead of buying a mainframe goat for dumb goats.
  • ISO won’t publish a standard for your herd.
  • Counting from zero and hexadecimal don’t apply (and look silly in a barn).
  • When you sell a goat, there’s no export format for legacy goat readers.
  • Everything still works at 100 goats, 256 goats—even 65,536 goats… probably.
  • Party small talk rarely involves “I just got a French Alpine, how do I milk it?”
  • No one can rummage through your goat and get you fired.
  • No user‑acceptance test required to deliver goat cheese.
  • No special utilities needed to delete a non‑empty goat.
  • You don’t need goats on your desktop—or shortcuts to goats. Most farmers don’t have desktops.
  • Nothing in farming requires a mouse. If you have mice, you get a cat.
  • Error messages are simple: Goat not found. Goat dead. Goat asleep. Too soon after last milking. Billy detected.
  • No read‑only oats. No hidden or system goats.
  • No core dumps to email for fixes—unless you want to cause a problem.
  • Goats don’t need to know the time to work.
  • Non‑Y2K‑compliant goats simply think it isn’t Y2K. No docs required.
  • Spouse vetoes a new goat? Encourage the herd to manufacture one from existing parts.
  • Goats don’t have enough fingers for Shift+Shift+Ctrl+Alt+Esc.
  • Blame games don’t last; goats just kick each other’s asses.
  • If goats documented every time they pooped, we’d be out of forests.
  • Goats don’t care about email.
  • The only “application delivery” a goat offers is… through the back end.
  • Goats can’t have benefits revoked; they become dinner instead.
  • No compliance report needed to prove milk throughput post‑2000.
  • GoatEng.
  • Macintosh‑goat users won’t mock you; their goats are just as problematic.
  • Even Perot voters end up with basically the same goats; now they can go back to arguing politics like it’s pre‑1984.
